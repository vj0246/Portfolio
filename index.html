01
MindVault ● Live

Upload documents and query them in plain English. Full-stack RAG system with multi-document routing, session memory, and an interactive knowledge graph.

PythonLangChain LCEL FAISSNext.jsSupabase
View details ↓ Close ↑
What it does

Upload any document and ask questions about it in plain English. MindVault finds the most relevant passages, routes your query across multiple documents intelligently, and remembers earlier parts of your conversation. It also builds a knowledge graph that maps how concepts across all your documents connect visually, a second way to navigate beyond chat.

Multi-user with Supabase authentication. Each user has their own private document space with row-level security enforced at the database level, not in application code.

How it was built, and why twice

Built the retrieval pipeline twice on purpose. First entirely from scratch without LangChain to understand every decision at the implementation level. Then rebuilt with LangChain LCEL RouterChain once the internals were clear. The key problem found during the manual build: without a similarity threshold the retriever answers confidently from irrelevant document chunks. Setting score_threshold=0.72 was the fix that turned it from a demo into something reliable.

Knowledge graph layer runs in parallel: entity and relation extraction runs over the full document text, stored as a graph, rendered as an interactive node view (GraphPanel.tsx + GraphExplorer.tsx in Next.js frontend). FastAPI backend handles ingestion, embedding, and LLM routing.
Technical depth
  • RouterChain with per-document FAISS retrievers, each uploaded document gets its own retriever; the router selects based on query-document cosine similarity before calling the LLM, preventing cross-document contamination
  • Similarity threshold gates retrieval, below 0.72, the system returns nothing and falls back rather than hallucinating from unrelated chunks
  • Session memory with rolling summarisation, past turns are compressed into a summary so context persists across long conversations without hitting context window limits
  • chunk_size=500 chosen empirically for dense typed notes, larger chunks diluted the meaning of technical content; smaller chunks lost context
  • Knowledge graph as a separate layer, entity/relation extraction runs independently from the retrieval pipeline so both views (chat and graph) stay in sync
  • Supabase row-level security, every query scoped to the authenticated user's document IDs at the database level

ApplyPilot In Development

An AI agent that handles the job-application grind. Upload resume, paste a job posting, and 15 seconds later get a cover letter, application email, fit score, reworded resume bullets, and grounded answers to form questions, all written so they don't read like a robot wrote them.

Next.js 14FastAPI Groq / Llama 3.3 70BPostgreSQL Docker
View details ↓ Close ↑
What it does

A working end-to-end product, not a demo. Upload a resume (PDF or DOCX) and AI extracts the full profile with an ATS score. Paste any job URL (Greenhouse, Lever, LinkedIn) or raw text and AI extracts requirements, salary, and culture signals. Hit apply and one background job produces a fit score, cover letter, application email, and adapted resume bullets. A form filler answers any application's custom questions with grounded, specific responses. Everything tracked through a real status pipeline: ready, approved, submitted, interviewing, offered.

Built for myself first, after writing the same cover letter 40 times with tiny tweaks. The AI gets you 90% there; you own the last 10% before hitting send.

Three-tier app: Next.js 14 frontend, FastAPI backend, Postgres storage. Fit score is pure Python (transparent skill-set intersection, no black-box embeddings). Three Groq calls run inside one FastAPI BackgroundTask, no Celery or Redis queue. Frontend polls every 2.5s while processing.
Key decisions and the pivot
  • Scrapped the enterprise stack: first pass used LangGraph + Celery + Redis + MinIO + Ollama (five extra containers, a broker to debug, a local LLM needing real hardware). Cut to FastAPI + Postgres + BackgroundTasks, three containers, one command to run. Swapping in Celery later is a contained change, not architecturally locked
  • Groq over OpenAI and over Ollama: OpenAI needs a paid key (dead on arrival for open source), Ollama needs 8GB+ RAM and a multi-GB download. Groq's free tier gives free, fast, zero-setup inference on Llama 3.3 70B
  • No vector DB, no embeddings: skill-set intersection is transparent (you see exactly why your fit score is what it is) where embedding similarity would be a black box that's harder to trust and debug
  • The AI never invents experience: adapt_resume_for_job() has explicit prompt rules to never add a skill or change a date, only reword existing bullets. Tested by feeding it a resume missing an obvious JD keyword to confirm it reframes rather than fabricates. A tool that makes you look qualified for something you're not is a liability, not a feature
  • Background tasks poll, not push: polling every 2.5s instead of WebSockets means less code and no extra connection to manage, and the latency doesn't matter for a 15-20s job

R1
Cost-Sensitive Deep Learning for Credit Scoring ACM Research · 2025

A novel deep learning architecture that learns non-linear latent borrower risk embeddings, models temporal debt acceleration, optimises for economic loss (not AUC), and produces regulator-grade explanations, built-in, not post-hoc.

Deep LearningCredit Risk Cost-Sensitive LearningXAI Latent EmbeddingsFinTech
View details ↓ Close ↑
The problem

Credit scoring sits at an intersection of three unsolved problems. First, high-performing deep models learn complex non-linear borrower representations that are impossible to explain to risk teams or satisfy regulators, explainability is now a legal requirement under frameworks like Basel and GDPR, and post-hoc methods like SHAP produce approximations, not faithful explanations. Second, misclassification costs in lending are fundamentally asymmetric and instance-dependent: rejecting a creditworthy borrower costs differently to approving a defaulter, and that ratio varies per loan. Optimising for AUC ignores this entirely. Third, existing models treat borrower risk as stationary, they miss that financial distress typically accelerates non-linearly before a default event.

This research addresses all three with one unified architecture: learn latent risk embeddings, explicitly model debt acceleration dynamics, optimise directly on economic loss with an instance-dependent cost function, and keep the decision process inherently interpretable.

The cost-sensitive decision layer uses an instance-dependent asymmetric loss, the false negative penalty (approving a defaulter) vs. false positive penalty (rejecting a good borrower) is not a global constant but varies per borrower based on loan amount and risk profile. The explainability layer is inherently interpretable by design: predictions decompose into feature-level contributions directly from the architecture's forward pass, not SHAP approximations.
Three original contributions
  • Non-linear latent risk embeddings, the model learns a borrower risk manifold from raw financial and behavioural inputs rather than relying on hand-crafted credit features, capturing interaction effects that linear scorecards cannot represent
  • Temporal debt acceleration modelling, explicitly models the rate of change of debt accumulation over time; financial distress rarely appears suddenly, it accelerates, and this module learns those pre-default dynamics from borrower history
  • Instance-dependent cost-sensitive optimisation, trained directly on economic loss where the asymmetric cost ratio is per-borrower, not a fixed global hyperparameter; makes the learning objective match what a lender actually cares about
  • Inherently interpretable architecture, explainability is built into the model's forward computation, not added as a post-hoc approximation; explanations are faithful to what the model actually computed, satisfying regulatory interpretability requirements
Why AUC is the wrong metric here

A model that correctly identifies 90% of defaults but misclassifies every large-loan defaulter as creditworthy scores well on AUC and is catastrophic in practice. This research trains directly on business loss: the asymmetric financial cost of each misclassification type, weighted by the specific borrower's loan profile. The evaluation metric is expected economic loss on the held-out set, not AUC.
